\begin{tcolorbox}[title=Question 1]
    Use the Intermediate Value theorem to find an interval of length one that
    contains a root of the equation, 
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
        \item $x^3 = 9$
        \item $\ln x + x^2 = 3$
    \end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox} 
Guess an root exists at [2,3]
\begin{align*}
    f(2) &= 8 - 9 = -1 \\
    f(3) &= 27 - 9 = 18 \\
.\end{align*}
By IVT, a root exists within that interval.
Do the same thing for the second part: We guess an interval of [1,2]
\begin{align*}
    f(1) &= \ln 1 + 1 - 3  =-2 \\
    f(2) &=  \ln 2 + 4 - 3 > 0 
.\end{align*}
By IVT, a root exists within that interval.

.\end{align*}

\begin{tcolorbox}[title=Question 4]
    Give an example to show that the eigenvalues can be changed when a multiple
    of one row is subtracted from another. Why is a zero eigenvalue not changed
    by the steps of elimination?
\end{tcolorbox} 
Take the matrix $A$ from Q4. Suppose that we perform  $R_2 \to  R_2 - 2R_1$ on the
matrix. We would have \[
    \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 6 \end{pmatrix} 
.\] 
Should we calculate the eigenvalue, we would get $\lambda \in \{1,6\}$, clearly
different than the original result.

To explain the reasoning behind the zero eigenvalues not being affected, we'll
go back to the definition of eigenvalues and eigenvectors. For a matrix $A$ with
an eigenvalue $\lambda = 0$, we have \[
    \det(A - \lambda I) = 0
.\] 
For any $\lambda \neq 0$, an elimination would shift the determinant of $A$ such
that the value of $\lambda$ would need to compensate. For a $\lambda = 0$
however,  $\det(A)$ is already $0$ (singular), and so any row operations would
not change the determinants, and $\lambda$ need not compensate.

\begin{tcolorbox}[title=Question 5]
    Compute $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ and their eigenvalues and unit eigenvectors for
    \[
    A = \begin{pmatrix} 
        1 & 1 & 0 \\ 
        0 & 1 & 1 \\
    \end{pmatrix} 
    .\] 
    Use your result to factor $A = U \Sigma V^T$
\end{tcolorbox} 

For $A^{T}A$, we have 
\begin{align*}
    A^{T}A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = 
    \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \\
    \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 - \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1
    - \lambda \end{pmatrix}
                           = (1 - \lambda)^2 (2 - \lambda) - 2(1-\lambda) = 0
.\end{align*}
\[
\lambda \in \{0,1,3\} 
.\] 
Calculating the unit eigenvectors, we have 
\[
v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} }\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix},
v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} 
v_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} }\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} 
.\] 
\[
V = 
\begin{pmatrix} 
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } \\ 
    -\frac{2}{\sqrt{6} } & 0 & \frac{2}{\sqrt{6} } \\
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} }    
\end{pmatrix} 
.\] 

Calculating $U$, 
 \[
     v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} 
     v_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3} } \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 3
     \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} 
.\] 
\[
    U = 
    \begin{pmatrix} 
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \\ 
        -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }}     
    \end{pmatrix} 
.\] 

Putting it all together, we have 
\[
A = 
    \begin{pmatrix} 
        \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } \\ 
        -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} }}     
    \end{pmatrix} 
    \begin{pmatrix} 
        \sqrt{3} & 0 & 0 \\ 
        0 & 1 & 0 \\
    \end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix} 
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } \\ 
    -\frac{2}{\sqrt{6} } & 0 & \frac{2}{\sqrt{6} } \\
    \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} } & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2} } & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6} }    
\end{pmatrix}
.\]

\[
