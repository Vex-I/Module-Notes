\section{Interpolation}
We'll start out with polynomial interpolation, which is basically try to fit a
polynomial such that it matches the given data points (we'll skip over trig and
rational interpolations in this course.)

In general, for a polynomial of degree $d$, a solution exists when the number of
the given data points is no more than $d + 1$, and the solution is unique as
long as there is exactly $d+1$ points. Computing the polynomial depends on the
basis matrix. One can choose any basis, each with their own advantages and
disadvatages. Here, we'll be covering the monomial, Langrange and Newton basis.

In general, a monomial basis is computaionally unstable due to ill conditioned
matrices, but conceptually, they are quite simple. Likewise, the Langrange basis
is easy to implement, but computationally expensive, and quite rigid, requiring
recalculating the whole thing for any additional points. Newton is the preferred
method, as well will see.

I'll start with the monomial basis matrix, also known as the
Vandermonde matrix. It looks something like this: \[
X = \begin{pmatrix} 
    1 & x_0 & x_0^2 & \cdots & x_0^d \\
    1 & x_1 & x_1^2 & \cdots & x_1^d \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    1 & x_n & x_n^2 & \cdots & x_n^d \\
\end{pmatrix} 
= \begin{pmatrix} 
    M_0(x_0) & M_1(x_0) & M_2(x_0) & \cdots & M_d(x_0) \\
    M_0(x_1) & M_1(x_1) & M_2(x_1) & \cdots & M_d(x_1) \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    M_0(x_n) & M_1(x_n) & M_2(x_n) & \cdots & M_d(x_n) \\
\end{pmatrix} 
.\] 
This looks like 
\begin{align*}
    p(x) &= a_0 + a_1x^2+ \ldots + a_n x^d \\
    &=  X \overline{a}
.\end{align*}

Find the polynomial that fit the points $(-2,-27),(0,-1),(1,0)$.
\[
\begin{pmatrix} 
    1 & x_0 & x_0^2 \\
    1 & x_1 & x_1^2 \\
    1 & x_2 & x_2^2 \\
\end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} 
=\begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \end{pmatrix} 
.\] 
\[
\begin{pmatrix} 
    1 & -2 & 4  \\
    1 & 0 & 0 \\
    1 & 1 & 1 \\
\end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} 
=
\begin{pmatrix} -27 \\ -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}

Let's consider another basis: the Langrange Basis. It is defined as follows:
Suppose that you have some points $(x_1,x_1), \ldots, (x_{n}, y_{n})$. For each
of these points, we form the function \[
\ell_i (x) = \prod_{k = 0, k \neq i}^{n} \frac{x - x_k}{x_{i} - x_k}  
.\] 
Then, we form the polynomial \[
P(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{n} y_{i} \ell_i (x)
.\] 
In matrix form, this is the equivalent of the system \[
    P(x) = L y^{T}
.\] 
\[
P(x) = \begin{pmatrix} \ell_1 \\ \vdots \\ \ell_n \end{pmatrix} 
\begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{pmatrix}^{T}
.\] 
Let's take a look at $\ell_i (x)$ first. We can see this as an indicator
function, that takes on the value of $1$ when $x = x_i$ and $0$ otherwise. From
here, we can see that $P(x)$  constructed from a langrange basis is simply the
summation of these indicator functions terms scaled by the corresponding value
of $y_i$. What you would arrive at is a continuous polynomial that returns the
value of $y_i$ at $x_i$.

\paragraph{Example} Take the example for the Vandermonde matrix. We have 
\begin{align*}
    \ell_1 (x) &= \frac{x(x-1)}{6} \\
    \ell_2 (x) &= \frac{(x + 2)(x-1)}{-2}  \\
    \ell_3 (x) &= \frac{(x + 2)x}{-1}  \\
    P(x) &= -\frac{27}{6}x(x-1) + \frac{1}{2}(x+2)(x-1)
.\end{align*}
